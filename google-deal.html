The deal that shouldn't have closed.

Dreamship was less than a year old. The counterparty was Google. What we built wasn't an acquisition and it wasn't a sale — it was a first-of-its-kind partnership that solved a problem Google actually had, became an 8-figure channel, and turned into a flywheel everyone else wanted on.

01The opening

A door, through a director.

The first contact came the way the best ones do — through a person, not a portal. An executive connection, a director inside Google, made the introduction. What got us in the room was specific: Dreamship had built a trademark and IP-compliance checker on Google Vision technology. What Google wanted was access to a market it couldn't safely reach on its own — high-volume international merchants — in a way that protected the Google brand.

That was the whole unlock. We weren't selling Google software. We were handing them a population of merchants who were verified, validated, and trustworthy — sellers who would drive real revenue through Google's ad platform without dragging brand risk in behind them. Dreamship became the trust layer between a frontier platform and the long tail it couldn't underwrite itself.

"We didn't sell to Google. We built something Google hadn't done before — and made it a win for them, for the merchants, and for us."

— Bill Bricker
02The room

The job was the incentives.

People imagine these deals turn on a single dramatic moment. This one didn't almost die. What it required was harder to point at: a message that was passionate, persuasive, and genuinely committed, and then the patient work of structuring the incentives so every party in the room won — Google, the merchants, and Dreamship. Get that geometry right and the deal stops being a negotiation and starts being obvious.

Closing it as a sub-one-year-old startup meant navigating 5–6 internal Google teams — trust & safety, business integrity, commercial — all the way to director-level sign-off. That's not a sales cycle; that's an organizational campaign. The output was an 8-figure ad-spend channel that ran as a win-win arrangement for 5+ years.

03The flywheel

Credibility compounds.

Once Google was real, the same value proposition — verified merchants, brand-safe access, aligned incentives — became repeatable. We sequenced partnerships with Stripe, PayPal, Payoneer, and Meta off the Google credibility. Each one wanted the same thing: a clean, trustworthy channel into international sellers they couldn't reach safely on their own.

This is the part I'd underline for anyone evaluating the work. The Google deal wasn't a trophy — it was the first node in a multi-party BD network. The durable skill on display isn't "closed a big logo." It's designing complex, multi-party deals where the incentives are aligned well enough that the next four come to you.

"There's something in creating business-development deals that are multi-party, complex, and have the incentives aligned. That's the pattern. Google was just the first one."

— Bill Bricker
04The engine underneath

11x, and why it held.

The partnerships gave us the channel; execution turned it into a business. The 11x growth year was our first full year — a little COVID tailwind, and a lot of simply capitalizing on the opening: taking in orders, figuring out supply routes, and meeting demand we'd uncovered. We turned a moment of access into a trusted source for reliable fulfillment, and the company crossed $35M+ in cumulative revenue. None of it was one decision. It was a set of decisions, made fast, by a capable team.

The part most people lead with last

I cut the Microsoft deal from a chemo chair.

In 2023 I stepped back from CEO to Board Chair after a cancer diagnosis, and did chemo through 2025. Mid-treatment, from a chair at Duke hospital, I used the same connector playbook to get into Microsoft just as it was standing up its ChatGPT-era Bing. We built a rev-share / go-to-market partnership that gave our merchants access to the new capability, negotiated it with senior Microsoft people, and graduated Dreamship into a higher partner tier on volume. The pipeline kept moving. The operator instinct gets ruthlessly clear when the day is short.
