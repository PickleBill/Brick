The brief in one breath

Bill is not a résumé. He is a person who builds in public, connects in public, and keeps going in public. The site should be the artifact of that, not a description of it.

resume website SaaS landing page dark neon AI site LinkedIn clone founder vanity
living artifact story map public OS proof-of-work

Three directions follow. Each is a complete world — not a moodboard, not a wireframe variant. They share an underlying point of view: that Bill’s edge is the seam between operator, builder, connector, and human in motion. Where they differ is the rhetorical posture they take toward that seam.

Direction I stages it as a system you can poke at. Direction II stages it as an editor’s desk you read from. Direction III stages it as a hand-drawn terrain you wander through. Each is buildable in Lovable in roughly a weekend; each rewards the visitor differently.

Pick one. Or pick the dimensions you want from each and we’ll fuse a fourth. The Lovable prompts at the foot of each section are paste-ready.

Visual system

Layout — Single-screen workspace shell. Left rail (file system), main pane (open file), right rail (live telemetry). Files open in tabs; the URL is the path. No scroll-to-marketing.

Type — Editorial serif (Spectral or GT Sectra) for human content; mono (JetBrains, Berkeley) for system chrome. Italic headlines. No display sans.

Color — Bone shell with slate ink. One signal color (lime or amber) reserved for “live” states only. No gradients.

Motion — Idle: cursor blink, telemetry pulse, uptime tick. Interactive: window-snap on open, terminal type-on for new entries.

Key interaction model

Every node in Bill’s life is a file. Visitors browse like they would a teammate’s laptop: open courtana/one-pager.mdx, open field_notes/2024-04.mdx, run ping bill in the terminal to send him a real message. A persistent “status: in the room” line is editable by Bill from his phone — it changes the room.

  • Heroopen workspace, no marketing
  • Navfile tree, not links
  • CTAterminal: $ ping bill
  • Updatescommit log, public

Best audience

Founders, operators, and technical investors who pattern-match credibility from artifacts, not adjectives. People who’d rather see a commit history than read a bio.

Why it might fail

The metaphor is loud. If executed thinly it reads as a gimmick. Demands real content density — an empty file system is worse than no site. Less legible to non-technical hiring managers; needs a one-key “Plain English” toggle.

Where the warmth lives

In the /life directory and in the field notes. The system is cold; the files are unguarded. Cancer journey lives here as The Long Walk, a real folder with real entries, not a CTA section.

On building Courtana while the rest of the room is still defining the problem.

A dispatch by the editor · 4 min read

“What I keep learning is that zero-to-one isn’t a phase you graduate from. It’s a posture. You either keep the muscle warm or you don’t.”

Three quarters ago this section was a different essay, on a different company, with a different roadmap. The work hasn’t changed shape so much as it’s changed weight. Where we used to start with a pitch and reverse into the build, we now start with the build and let the pitch follow it home. The room is faster. The room is also more crowded. What that means in practice is that the connectors — people who can hold five strangers in their head at once and make them useful to each other — have stopped being a nice-to-have. They are now infrastructure. This issue tries to make a small honest map of what that looks like, week by week, in one operator’s working life.

Visual system

Layout — A masthead-driven editorial page. Vol. / No. / Season. A lead essay, three numbered dispatches in a sidebar, a quiet archive at the foot. Generous gutters, justified columns, drop caps, hanging numerals.

Type — One serif family doing nearly all of the work (Spectral, GT Sectra, or Source Serif), with a small mono for foliate metadata. Italic for voice.

Color — Vellum paper, walnut ink, one foil-bronze accent for issue numbers and pull quotes. Nothing else.

Motion — Almost none. Pages turn (subtle slide) when changing issue. Pull quotes fade in from baseline. The site feels printed.

Key interaction model

The site is an issue. Bill publishes a new issue every season — the homepage is the current issue. Old issues live in an archive that reads like a bookshelf spine. Each issue has a lead essay, three dispatches, and one personal letter. A discreet “working with Bill” page sits one click away — written as a colophon, not a sales page.

  • Heromasthead + lead essay
  • Navthis issue / archive / colophon
  • CTAsubscribe (one email per season)
  • Cadencefour real updates a year

Best audience

Boards, LPs, late-stage founders, executive recruiters, family-office types. People who decide based on the quality of someone’s prose as a proxy for the quality of their thinking. The Monocle/Stripe Press reader.

Why it might fail

Demands real writing on a real cadence. If Bill misses two issues, the artifact decays in public. Less obviously “AI-native” on first glance — the proof is in the prose, not the chrome. Younger founders may misread “refined” as “stuffy.”

Where the warmth lives

In the third dispatch of every issue: A short letter from—. The cancer journey is one of these letters, not a section. The form holds the weight because it’s the form a thoughtful person uses for thoughtful things.

Visual system

Layout — A single large canvas the visitor pans and zooms. Regions are clusters: Building, Selling, Connecting, Home, The Long Walk. Every artifact is a placed pin on the territory.

Type — A warm humanist serif (Spectral italic) for place names, a hand script (Caveat or a custom marker) for marginalia, mono only in the legend.

Color — Paper-ochre ground, sienna ink, one cool blue reserved for the “river of intros.” Hand-drawn texture, soft paper grain, no flat fills.

Motion — Soft parallax on pan. Pins breathe slowly. Hovering a region drops everything else to 30% opacity. Trails animate-draw on first visit only.

Key interaction model

The visitor wanders. There is no nav. Pinch/scroll to zoom, drag to pan. Tapping a pin opens a small hand-titled card with a real story — what it is, what was hard, what it taught him, what he’d do again. A compass in the corner lets you fast-travel to a named region. Bill adds pins from his phone; the map grows visibly.

  • Herothe territory itself
  • Navcompass + zoom
  • CTA“leave a note in the margin”
  • Updatesnew pins, never removed

Best audience

Mission-aligned founders, community-builders, values-driven operators, friends-of-friends arriving through a warm intro. People who hire on character first and credentials second. The kind of person Bill actually wants in his life.

Why it might fail

The least “serious” on first glance — an investor with 90 seconds may not pan past the title. Needs a tight default zoom that already shows three real proof points. Map metaphors can drift into cute; the writing has to hold the line.

Where the warmth lives

Everywhere — that’s the whole point. Home · Three kids is a pin, not a footer line. The Long Walk is a literal trail across the map you can follow with your finger.

How to choose

Pick I if the primary audience is operators and builders. Pick II if it’s boards, LPs, and senior recruiters. Pick III if Bill wants the site to do the warm-intro work it’s currently doing in DMs.

What I’d do next
  1. Bill picks one direction (or two dimensions to fuse).
  2. I spec the content inventory — what files / dispatches / pins must exist on day one.
  3. You paste the prompt into Lovable; I clean up the output and supply the writing.
One honest note

All three concepts assume Bill writes the words. The artifact only feels alive because the voice is. We can ghost-draft seeds; we cannot ghost the cadence.
